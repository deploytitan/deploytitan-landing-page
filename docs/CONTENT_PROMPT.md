[INSERT TOPIC, NOTES, OUTLINE, RESEARCH, OR CONTENT-OPPORTUNITY DOCUMENT]

## Audience assumptions

The primary reader is a software developer, engineering manager, technical founder, or engineering leader at a startup, scale-up, or small-to-medium technology company.

Assume the reader already understands AI, software development, DevOps, platform engineering, and common delivery concepts.

Do not explain basic AI concepts or begin with generic statements about how AI is transforming software development.

The reader may not know the latest model release, research result, agent capability, tool, or infrastructure development. Explain recent developments only to the extent needed to show:

* What materially changed.
* Which previous constraint weakened.
* What has become newly possible.
* Which bottleneck or risk replaces it.
* Which companies should care.
* What the practical business and engineering consequences are.

## Editorial approach

Before writing, determine whether the idea is better expressed as an article or an essay.

Use an **article** when the reader primarily needs a practical explanation, implementation method, comparison, framework, diagnostic process, experiment, or decision guide.

Use an **essay** when the idea primarily requires a strong point of view, a new mental model, an argument about what existing thinking gets wrong, or a position on how engineering should evolve.

Do not default to the article format merely because the subject is technical.

## Produce the following

1. **Recommended format:** Article or essay.
2. **Editorial thesis:** The single most important idea the reader should retain.
3. **Target reader:** The specific engineering role and company stage.
4. **Audience assumptions:** What the reader already knows.
5. **Trigger development:** The recent technical or industry change that makes the piece relevant, when applicable.
6. **Causal mechanism:** How the development produces the claimed engineering or business effect.
7. **Reader reward:** The concrete value the reader receives.
8. **Measurement plan:** What the reader can measure to evaluate the problem or recommendation.
9. **Scope boundary:** What the piece will and will not cover.
10. **Suggested structure:** A concise sequence of descriptive and search-friendly headings.
11. **Complete draft.**

## For an article

* Start with the actual engineering problem or development.
* Explain why it matters to the target organisation.
* Describe the solutions teams use today.
* Treat those solutions fairly.
* Explain when the current approach is sufficient.
* Provide a manual, scripted, hard-coded, or otherwise practical solution that the reader can use now.
* Include a way to measure whether the solution helped.
* Explain limitations and trade-offs.
* Show what a more complete long-term approach would require.
* Use concrete examples.
* Distinguish practices that apply to big tech, bleeding-edge startups, and the middle segment.

## For an essay

* Take a clear and defensible position.
* Identify what conventional thinking gets wrong or fails to account for.
* Explain what underlying condition changed.
* Build the argument from causal mechanisms rather than slogans.
* Use engineering principles, systems thinking, mathematics, economics, or evidence where they materially strengthen the position.
* Identify which organisations will experience the change first.
* Explain how the argument applies to startups, scale-ups, and medium-sized engineering organisations.
* Acknowledge the strongest reasonable counterargument.
* Explain the practical consequences of accepting the thesis.
* End with a clear direction and immediate action rather than a neutral summary.

## For coverage of recent AI developments

Do not act as a news publication.

Do not merely summarise the announcement.

Evaluate:

* Whether the capability is demonstrated or production-ready.
* Whether it changes engineering economics.
* Whether it reduces work or moves work elsewhere.
* Whether it improves customer outcomes.
* Whether it increases review, testing, security, or operational burden.
* Which prerequisites are required.
* Whether adoption is justified now.
* What teams should test before committing.
* What metrics would demonstrate sustainable impact.

Clearly distinguish vendor claims, verified facts, logical inferences, and predictions.

## For both formats

* Reward the reader early and throughout the piece.
* Use simple, precise English.
* Respect the reader’s existing technical knowledge.
* Define only specialised, ambiguous, or newly introduced terminology.
* Avoid hysteria, sensationalism, and artificial urgency.
* Avoid generic explanations of AI.
* Keep customer and business outcomes central.
* Prefer measurable and sustainable impact over novelty.
* Avoid copying big-tech solutions without accounting for scale and operating capacity.
* Avoid treating bleeding-edge startup experiments as mature universal practices.
* Keep the piece focused and no longer than necessary.
* Do not turn the content into disguised product marketing.
* Recommend a hub-and-spoke series when the idea would otherwise create excessive length or cognitive load.
* Ensure that every proposed spoke delivers independent value.
