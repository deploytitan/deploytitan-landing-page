Using the DT Content Soul as your editorial standard, develop the following content idea:

[INSERT TOPIC, NOTES, OUTLINE, OR CONTENT-OPPORTUNITY DOCUMENT]

Before writing, determine whether this idea is better expressed as an article or an essay.

Use an article when the reader primarily needs a practical explanation, solution, implementation method, comparison, framework, or decision guide.

Use an essay when the idea primarily requires a strong point of view, an argument about what existing thinking gets wrong, or a position on how engineering should evolve.

Then provide:

1. **Recommended format:** Article or essay.
2. **Editorial thesis:** The single most important idea the reader should retain.
3. **Target reader:** Who this is for and what they likely already understand.
4. **Reader reward:** The concrete value the reader receives for finishing the piece.
5. **Scope boundary:** What the piece will and will not attempt to cover.
6. **Suggested structure:** A concise sequence of descriptive, search-friendly headings.
7. **Complete draft.**

For an article:

* Explain the problem and why it occurs.
* Describe the solutions teams use today.
* Treat those solutions fairly.
* Provide a manual, scripted, hard-coded, or otherwise practical solution that the reader can use now.
* Explain the limitations and trade-offs.
* Show what a more complete long-term solution would require.
* Include concrete examples.

For an essay:

* Take a clear and defensible position.
* Identify what conventional thinking gets wrong or fails to account for.
* Build the argument from engineering mechanisms rather than slogans.
* Use examples, systems thinking, mathematics, economics, or empirical evidence where they materially improve the argument.
* Acknowledge the strongest counterargument.
* Explain the practical consequences of accepting the thesis.
* End with a clear direction rather than a neutral summary.

For both formats:

* Reward the reader early and throughout the piece.
* Use simple, precise English.
* Keep the piece focused and shorter than a typical exhaustive guide.
* Define necessary jargon.
* Avoid generic introductions and repetitive conclusions.
* Do not turn the piece into disguised product marketing.
* Clearly separate facts, interpretations, hypotheses, and predictions.
* Recommend splitting the idea into a hub-and-spoke series when one piece would create excessive length or cognitive load.
* Ensure that every proposed spoke is independently useful.
